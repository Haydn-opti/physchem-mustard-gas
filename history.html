Chapter 03

WWI & Chemical Warfare

Mustard gas didn't just injure soldiers — it fundamentally changed how wars were fought. Its persistence turned it from a weapon into a tool of area denial, forcing armies to abandon entire positions.

The Chemical Arms Race

By 1917, chemical weapons had already been part of the war for two years. Chlorine gas (first used at Ypres in 1915) and phosgene were both deployed as lethal respiratory agents. But both shared a critical limitation: they were gases that dispersed quickly in the wind, meaning their tactical effect was unpredictable and short-lived.[7]

Mustard gas changed the equation entirely. As a persistent liquid with extremely low volatility, it didn't blow away — it soaked into everything it touched and remained dangerous for days or weeks.

Timeline

April 1915

First Large-Scale Chemical Attack

Germany releases chlorine gas at the Second Battle of Ypres — the first major use of chemical weapons in modern warfare. The gas dissipates within minutes, limiting its tactical effectiveness.[7]

December 1915

Phosgene Introduced

Phosgene, roughly 18× more lethal than chlorine by concentration, enters the battlefield. It is harder to detect but still disperses rapidly as a gas.[7]

July 12, 1917

Mustard Gas Deployed for the First Time

The German army fires mustard gas artillery shells against British forces near Ypres, Belgium during the Third Battle of Ypres (Passchendaele). Unlike previous gases, the oily liquid contaminates the ground, trenches, and equipment for extended periods.[7]

1917–1918

Rapid Escalation

Both the Allied and Central powers ramp up production. By the war's end, tens of thousands of tons of mustard gas had been manufactured and deployed across the Western Front.[7]

November 1918

Armistice

The war ends with chemical weapons having caused an estimated 1.3 million casualties total (including all agents). Mustard gas alone accounted for the majority of chemical injuries in the final year of the war.[7]

Why Mustard Gas Changed Everything

Previous chemical agents were immediate-effect weapons — you could see the gas cloud, move out of the way, or put on a mask. Mustard gas broke every rule:

⏳

Delayed Onset

Symptoms appeared 2–24 hours after exposure. Soldiers didn't know they'd been hit until the damage was already done, making protective measures reactive rather than preventive.

🏗️

Area Denial

Liquid droplets soaked into soil, wood, and uniforms. Entire trench systems became uninhabitable for days, forcing armies to abandon strategically important positions.[7]

🛡️

Masks Weren't Enough

Gas masks protected the lungs but not the skin. Mustard gas penetrated clothing and caused severe blisters on any exposed surface, rendering respiratory protection insufficient.

⬇️

Heavier Than Air

With a vapor density 5.4× that of air, any vapors that did form sank directly into trenches and dugouts — exactly where soldiers were sheltering.[3]
