Chapter 02

The Chemistry

How mustard gas was mass-produced, why it behaves the way it does physically, and the step-by-step molecular mechanism through which it destroys living cells.

Industrial Synthesis: The Levinstein Process

During WWI, militaries needed to produce mustard gas rapidly and at scale. The Levinstein process became the preferred method because of its simplicity — it required only two readily available chemicals and no complex equipment.[1]

The reaction combines dry ethylene gas (C₂H₄) with sulfur monochloride (S₂Cl₂):

S₂Cl₂ + 2 C₂H₄ → (ClCH₂CH₂)₂S + S

Levinstein Process — Overall Balanced Equation

The product — often called "Levinstein mustard" — was only about 70% pure bis(2-chloroethyl) sulfide. The remaining 30% consisted of unreacted sulfur and bis(2-chloroethyl) polysulfide impurities, which gave the weaponized product its characteristic dark brown color and corrosive instability.[1]

Why impurities mattered: The polysulfide byproducts made the mixture more corrosive and unpredictable than pure mustard gas. The dark color also made contaminated areas visible, but the impurities increased skin irritation and made decontamination more difficult for soldiers.

Physical & Chemical Properties

The physical properties of mustard gas are a direct consequence of its molecular structure and intermolecular forces. Every property listed below connects back to the bonding and polarity discussed in the previous chapter.

Property Value Why It Matters
Appearance (pure) Colorless, oily liquid Impurities from synthesis turn it yellow-brown
Odor Garlic or mustard-like Odor threshold is above the level that causes harm — by the time you smell it, you've been exposed
Melting Point 13–14 °C Liquid at most battlefield temperatures
Boiling Point ~217 °C Very high for its size — caused by strong dipole-dipole and London dispersion forces
Vapor Pressure 0.09 mmHg at 30 °C Extremely low volatility — persists as liquid for days/weeks
Vapor Density 5.4× heavier than air Sinks into trenches and low-lying areas
Solubility in Water Practically insoluble Long nonpolar carbon chains make it hydrophobic
Lipophilicity Highly fat-soluble Penetrates skin and clothing rapidly

Structure → Properties: The high boiling point and low volatility trace directly to the strong London dispersion forces (large molar mass of 159 g/mol) and dipole-dipole interactions (polar molecule). The fat-solubility comes from the long hydrocarbon chains, making the molecule more nonpolar overall despite having polar ends.[3][4]

Biological Mechanism: How It Destroys Cells

Mustard gas doesn't just burn skin on contact — it causes damage at the molecular level by directly attacking and cross-linking DNA. This mechanism is what makes its injuries so severe, delayed, and difficult to treat.

1

Intramolecular Cyclization

Inside the body's aqueous environment, the sulfur atom's lone electron pair attacks the adjacent carbon atom, displacing the chlorine atom through an intramolecular nucleophilic substitution (SN1-type) reaction. This forms a highly reactive, positively charged three-membered ring called a cyclic sulfonium ion.[5]

2

DNA Alkylation

The electrophilic sulfonium ion is aggressively reactive. It seeks out nucleophilic sites on biological molecules, particularly the N7 nitrogen atom on guanine bases in DNA. The sulfonium ring opens as it forms a covalent bond with the guanine, permanently modifying the DNA strand.[5]

3

Cross-Linking

Because mustard gas has two chloroethyl arms, this entire process repeats on the second arm. The molecule binds to a guanine base on the opposite DNA strand, creating an interstrand DNA cross-link — physically locking the two strands of the double helix together.[5]

4

Cell Death

These irreversible cross-links prevent the DNA from unzipping for replication or transcription. The cell cannot divide or repair itself, leading to massive tissue blistering, destruction of rapidly dividing cells (skin, eyes, lungs, bone marrow), and ultimately programmed cell death (apoptosis).[5]

Why exposure is delayed: Mustard gas symptoms typically don't appear for 2–24 hours after exposure. This is because the molecular damage — the DNA cross-linking — takes time to cascade into visible cellular destruction. By the time blisters appear, irreversible damage has already been done at the DNA level.
