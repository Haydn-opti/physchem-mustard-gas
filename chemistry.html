Chapter 02

The Chemistry

How mustard gas was mass-produced, why it behaves the way it does physically, and the step-by-step molecular mechanism through which it destroys living cells.

Industrial Synthesis: The Levinstein Process

During WWI, militaries needed to produce mustard gas rapidly and at scale. The Levinstein process became the preferred method because of its simplicity, requiring only two readily available chemicals and no complex equipment.[1]

In this synthesis reaction, dry ethylene gas (C₂H₄) reacts with sulfur monochloride (S₂Cl₂) to form mustard gas:

S₂Cl₂ + 2 C₂H₄ → (ClCH₂CH₂)₂S + S

Levinstein Process: Overall Balanced Equation

The product — often called "Levinstein mustard" — was only about 70% pure bis(2-chloroethyl) sulfide. The remaining 30% consisted of unreacted sulfur and bis(2-chloroethyl) polysulfide impurities, which gave the weaponized product its characteristic dark brown color and corrosive instability.[1]

Why impurities mattered: The polysulfide byproducts made the mixture more corrosive and unpredictable than pure mustard gas, and while the dark color also made contaminated areas visibe, its instability increased skin irritation and made decontamination much more difficult for effected soldiers.

Physical & Chemical Properties

The physical properties of mustard gas are a direct consequence of its molecular structure and bonding.

Reaction with Water & Living Tissue

Why Skin Is Vulnerable

The lipophilicity noted in the properties table above is what makes mustard gas so effective at penetrating the body. Due to its high fat-soluble nature, it dissolves readily through the oily lipid layer of skin rather than being repelled by water-based defences such as sweat or mucus, reaching cells before the body can wash or dilute it away once absorbed[7]

Hydrolysis: Reacting with Water in the Skin

Once inside tissue, the C–Cl bonds react with water present in cells, causing a hydrolysis reaction, where water breaks down the chemical bonds in a molecule, for which the chlorine (Cl) on each arm trades places with a hydroxyl group (OH) from water, producing a hydroxy compound and hydrochloric acid (HCl).[7]

(ClCH₂CH₂)₂S + 2 H₂O → (HOCH₂CH₂)₂S + 2 HCl

Hydrolysis of Mustard Gas: Overall Balanced Equation

The molar ratio is 1 : 2 : 1 : 2: one molecule of mustard gas reacts with two molecules of water to produce one hydroxy compound and two molecules of hydrochloric acid, a strong proton donor that when released inside tissue, burns and destroys cells on contact.

Cross-Linking: Why the Damage Is So Severe

Mustard gas has two C–Cl bonds - one on each arm of the molecule. Both bonds can react, and this is what makes it particularly destructive: when the two arms react with two separate biological molecules — such as two protein chains or two strands of DNA — mustard gas acts as a bridge, forming covalent bonds with both and locking them together.

These cross-linked proteins can no longer fold into their correct shapes and cease to function, and cross-linked DNA strands can no longer be replicated. Because covalent bonds are strong, the body cannot easily break these links, causing damage to accumulate faster than cells can repair it..[3]

Outcome: Symptoms are often delayed by hours, with the damage building silently before pain is felt. Exposure causes severe blistering of the skin, damage to the eyes and airways, and in high doses, destruction of internal tissue, with even small amounts causing injuries that could take weeks to heal.[7]
