Chapter 01

The Molecule

Understanding mustard gas starts at the atomic level. Its geometry, bond types, and intermolecular forces explain everything from its physical persistence to its ability to destroy DNA.

Identity

Mustard gas, formally named bis(2-chloroethyl) sulfide, has the molecular formula C₄H₈Cl₂S and a molar mass of 159.08 g/mol.[2] The molecule is built around a central sulfur atom bonded to two identical chloroethyl chains (ClCH₂CH₂–), giving it a symmetric structure.

Property Value
IUPAC Name Bis(2-chloroethyl) sulfide
Molecular Formula C₄H₈Cl₂S
Molar Mass 159.08 g/mol
CAS Number 505-60-2
Common Names Mustard gas, sulfur mustard, HD, yperite

Bond Types

The bonds in mustard gas are all covalent (shared electrons), but they differ in how equally electrons are shared between the bonded atoms.

Bond Type Electron Sharing Significance
C–H Covalent Nearly equal Carbon and hydrogen have very similar electronegativities
C–S Covalent Slightly unequal Sulfur is slightly more electronegative than carbon
C–Cl Covalent Unequal Chlorine is highly electronegative

These bonds are key to mustard gas's reactivity and its ability to alkylate DNA.

Key takeaway: The C–Cl bonds at each end of the molecule are the reactive sites. Because chlorine draws shared electrons toward itself more strongly than carbon does, these bonds break more easily than C–C or C–H bonds — allowing mustard gas to react with and lock onto biological molecules. [3]
