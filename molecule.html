Chapter 01

The Molecule

Understanding mustard gas starts at the atomic level — its geometry, bond types, and intermolecular forces explain everything from its persistence on the battlefield to its ability to destroy DNA.

Identity

Mustard gas — formally named bis(2-chloroethyl) sulfide — has the molecular formula C₄H₈Cl₂S and a molar mass of 159.08 g/mol.[2] The molecule is built around a central sulfur atom bonded to two identical chloroethyl chains (ClCH₂CH₂–), giving it a symmetric structure.

Molecular Geometry

The shape of each atom's bonding environment determines mustard gas's overall 3D structure and, ultimately, its chemical behavior.

⚛️

Sulfur — Bent Geometry

The central sulfur atom has 2 bonding pairs and 2 lone electron pairs, resulting in a bent molecular geometry around sulfur. This bend is critical — it makes the molecule polar overall, since the two C–S bond dipoles don't cancel out.[2]

🔷

Carbon — Tetrahedral Geometry

Each of the four carbon atoms is sp³-hybridized with four bonding pairs and zero lone pairs, giving each carbon center a tetrahedral geometry with bond angles of approximately 109.5°.[2]

Bond Types

The bonds in mustard gas are all covalent (shared electrons), but they differ in polarity based on the electronegativity differences between the bonded atoms.

Bond Type Polarity Significance
C–H Covalent Nearly nonpolar Carbon and hydrogen have very similar electronegativities
C–S Covalent Slightly polar Sulfur is slightly more electronegative than carbon
C–Cl Covalent Polar Chlorine is highly electronegative — these bonds are the key to mustard gas's reactivity and its ability to alkylate DNA

Key takeaway: The highly polar C–Cl bonds at each end of the molecule are the reactive sites. In the body, the chlorine atoms are displaced through nucleophilic substitution — this is the mechanism that causes DNA cross-linking and cellular destruction.[5]

Polarity & Intermolecular Forces

Because of the bent geometry around sulfur and the strongly polar C–Cl bonds at the molecular extremities, mustard gas is an overall polar molecule. This polarity, combined with its large size, gives rise to two dominant intermolecular forces:[2]

⇄

Dipole–Dipole Interactions

The molecule's net dipole moment (from the bent geometry and polar bonds) causes neighboring molecules to attract through permanent electrostatic interactions between their positive and negative ends.

⟡

London Dispersion Forces

With a molar mass of 159.08 g/mol, mustard gas is a relatively large molecule. Its large electron cloud generates strong temporary dipoles, making London dispersion forces a significant contributor to its high boiling point and low volatility.[2]

Why this matters on the battlefield: These strong intermolecular forces are directly responsible for mustard gas's high boiling point (217 °C) and extremely low vapor pressure. This is why it persists in the environment as a liquid for days or weeks rather than evaporating quickly — unlike chlorine or phosgene, which dissipate in minutes.[3]
